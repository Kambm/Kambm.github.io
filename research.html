Random Flows

Many biological and physical problems can be cast naturally as problems of understanding the properties of random fluid flows. A particularly important feature of these flows is the topology of the streamlines. Closed streamlines recirculate, or 'trap' material, while open streamlines advect material to infinity. This distinction has consequences in a variety of systems modeled by random flows, from the mucociliary clearance system to electronic transport in disordered materials.

My work in this area has focused on developing tools to relate the statistics of the topological characteristics of random flows in two dimensions to the statstics of the underlying distribution from which they were generated.

Papers:

  1. Mason Kamb, Janie Byrum, Greg Huber, Guillaume Le Treut, Shalin Mehta, Boris Veytsman, David Yllanes. Statistical topology of the streamlines of a 2D flow, arXiv preprint arXiv:2110.10624 (2021).


Koopman Theory

Bernard Koopman showed in 1931 that the flow of any finite-dimensional nonlinear system could be associated with the action of an infinite-dimensional linear operator on certain Hilbert spaces of 'observable functions' on the state space. As linear systems are generally easier to study and control than nonlinear systems, a great amount of work has been done to develop tractable approximations of the action of this operator on well-chosen finite-dimensional subspaces of observables.

My work in this area has investigated the use of delay coordinates as a choice for such subspaces, investigating the analytical properties of the Koopman operator on these observables and discovering several universal, system-independent features of the action of the operator on these coordinates.

Papers:

  1. Mason Kamb, Eurika Kaiser, Steven L. Brunton, J. Nathan Kutz. Time-delay observables for koopman: Theory and applications, SIAM Journal on Applied Dynamical Systems, 19(2) (2020).


Human Impacts

The staggering breadth and scope of human activities is hard to comprehend. Enormous figures such as "a gigaton of carbon dioxide," "a Terawatt of power," or "a million hectares of forest loss" are difficult to grasp intuitively. Furthermore, the great diversity of human activities means that it is difficult to find unified perspectives on all facets of our impacts on the world. Motivated by this, we have developed a compendium of several areas of human activities, presenting each of these as a "dimensionless number"-- framing the magnitude of the human impact against the magnitude of a relatable natural process. In addition, we have compiled an easily-searchable database of > 250 numbers, cataloguing human impacts, ranging from annual emissions of CH4, to the area of the Antarctic ozone hole, to the global water volume used in irrigation.

Papers:

  1. Griffin Chure, Rachel Banks, Avi Flamholz, , Nicholas Sarai, Mason Kamb, Ignacio Lopez-Gomez, Yinon Bar-On, Ron Milo, Rob Phillips. The Anthropocene by the Numbers: A Quantitative Snapshot of Humanity's Influence on the Planet, arXiv preprint arXiv:2101.09620 (2021).


Epidemiology

During the COVID-19 pandemic, I worked with an interdisciplinary group at the Chan-Zuckerberg Biohub to develop models of infectious dynamics. This included developing a model to incorporate local clustering effects, describing households, schools and workplaces, as well as ongoing work to describe the effects of heterogeneous mobility on transmission.





Papers:

  1. Greg Huber, Mason Kamb, Kyle Kawagoe, Lucy M Li, Aaron McGeever, Jonathan Miller, Boris Veytsman, Dan Zigmond. A minimal model for household-based testing and tracing in epidemics, Physical Biology (2021).

  2. Greg Huber, Mason Kamb, Kyle Kawagoe, Lucy M. Li, Boris Veytsman, David Yllanes, Dan Zigmond. A minimal model for household effects in epidemics, Physical Biology (2020).
